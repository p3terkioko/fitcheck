Fitness Misinformation Detection System
Progress Update  ·  Milestone 2
Peter Kioko  ·  P15/144253/2022
The Problem

Fitness and nutrition content on TikTok and Instagram reaches millions daily. No automated system exists to verify whether the claims being made are supported by peer-reviewed science.

The Solution

A user pastes a social media URL. The system extracts the audio, transcribes it, identifies every verifiable fitness claim, and checks each one against 921 peer-reviewed papers — returning a verdict with confidence and citations.

Literature Review
ThemeKey FindingsSources
Misinformation Growth Exponential increase in health misinfo post-COVID; 88% of Kenyans encounter online misinformation Yeung et al. (2022); Wasserman & Madrid-Morales (2019)
Fitness-Specific Gaps Exercise misinfo mixes scientific jargon with unproven claims; only 31% of nutrition websites provide evidence-based content Marocolo et al. (2021); Denniss et al. (2023)
Detection Limitations Existing systems focus on political/medical content; 82–85% accuracy but lack fitness domain specialisation Wang et al. (2020); Schlicht et al. (2024)
Kenyan Context Rapid social media adoption outpaces digital literacy; cultural factors influence credibility assessment Tully (2022); Wasserman & Madrid-Morales (2019)
RAG & LLMs for Fact Verification Retrieval-Augmented Generation reduces hallucination and improves factual grounding; sentence embeddings enable semantic similarity search at scale Lewis et al. (2020); Reimers & Gurevych (2019)
